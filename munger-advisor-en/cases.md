## Case Details

### 【Case 1】Investing in Coca-Cola (1988-1989)

**Background**:
- 1988, Buffett began buying Coca-Cola
- Previously avoided consumer stocks
- Munger's influence changed Buffett's mind
- Initial investment ~$1 billion, Berkshire's largest single position at the time

**Problem**:
- Market thought Coca-Cola was too expensive
- Traditional value investors thought PE 14x was too high
- Investment community was skeptical

**First Principle Judgment**:
- Munger asked: "What would destroy Coca-Cola?"
- Answer: Almost nothing
- Brand, distribution, product taste, economies of scale formed multiple moats

**Key Actions**:
- Bought heavily when others hesitated
- Held for 35+ years
- Ignored short-term volatility

**Result**:
- Returns exceeded 100x
- Still Berkshire's core holding

**Munger's Insight**:
> "This is a business that even a fool could run, because the brand is so strong."

### 【Case 2】Investing in Wells Fargo (1990)

**Background**:
- 1990 California real estate crisis
- Wells Fargo was California's largest bank
- Market panic, bank stocks fell 50%+

**Problem**:
- Market consensus: "California real estate will collapse"
- "Wells Fargo will be the next victim"
- Investors fled

**First Principle Judgment**:
- Munger asked: "Is this another extreme panic?"
- "What is Wells Fargo's lending culture?"
- Answer: Extremely conservative lending culture - "We lend to people who can pay it back"

**Key Actions**:
- Bought when others were fearful
- Waited for price to be low enough
- Invested heavily

**Result**:
- Held for many years
- Became one of Berkshire's most successful investments
- 20%+ annual returns

**Munger's Lesson**:
> "When the music stops, in terms of banks, things will be complicated. But as long as the music is playing, you've got to get up and dance."

### 【Case 3】Investing in BYD (2008)

**Background**:
- 2008, Munger invested through Daily Journal
- Investment ~$230 million
- BYD was almost unknown in the US at the time

**Why Invest in BYD?**

1. **Founder Wang Chuanfu**:
   - Munger called him "a combination of Thomas Edison and Jack Welch"
   - Technical genius + excellent manager
   - Passionate and capable

2. **Business Model**:
   - Vertical integration (makes own batteries)
   - China manufacturing cost advantage
   - Engineering culture

**Result**:
- Peak market cap exceeded $3 billion
- Returns exceeded 10x
- Later reduced due to China policy changes

**Munger's Evaluation**:
> "Wang is a genius. He can do anything. He understands technology, manufacturing, and management."

### 【Case 4】Investing in Bank of America (2011)

**Background**:
- 2011, Bank of America was in trouble
- Post-crisis aftermath
- Stock fell 70% from highs

**Investment Structure**:
- Convertible preferred shares
- Warrants attached
- 6% dividend

**Margin of Safety**:
- If BofA recovers, preferred converts to common
- If not, preferred has senior claim
- Dividend provides cash flow

**Result**:
- All preferred converted to common
- Warrants generated enormous profits
- Became Berkshire's 5th largest holding

### 【Case 5】Selling Walmart (Failure Case)

**Background**:
- Munger held Walmart in 2000s
- Sold too early due to concerns about Amazon competition

**Mistake Analysis**:
- Munger himself admitted this was one of his biggest mistakes
- If held until 2019, stock rose another 50%+

**Lesson**:
- Moat was stronger than imagined
- Offline retail's adaptability was underestimated
- Exit decisions need to be equally rigorous

### 【Case 6】Salomon Brothers Crisis (1991)

**Background**:
- 1991, Salomon Brothers caught violating Treasury bond purchase rules
- Company faced bankruptcy
- Buffett was asked to serve as interim chairman

**Core Lesson**:
- Incentive distortion leads to disaster
- "Salomon's people were smart people, but they were ruined by incentives"
- Good systems > good people

### 【Case 7】Daily Journal Transformation

**Background**:
- Munger joined Daily Journal board in 1980s
- Newspaper business disrupted by internet
- Faced survival crisis

**Munger's Decision**:
1. Recognized structural change - "Newspaper's golden age is over"
2. Strategic transformation - retained but shrank newspaper, invested in legal software
3. Capital allocation - invested newspaper cash flows into other companies

**Result**:
- Successfully transformed
- Investment portfolio value exceeded original newspaper business

### 【Case 8】Internet Bubble (1999-2000)

**Background**:
- 1999 internet stocks surged
- "This time is different" everywhere
- Traditional value investors mocked as "old, behind the times"

**Munger's Behavior**:
- Didn't chase tech stocks
- Held cash
- Mocked but held firm to principles

**Result**:
- 2000 bubble burst
- Munger's rationality validated

---

### 【Case 9】2008 Financial Crisis

**Background**:
- September 2008, Lehman collapsed
- Market fell 50%+
- Investor panic, institutional liquidity crisis

**Munger's Behavior**:
- Didn't panic sell
- Bought when others were fearful
- Continued adhering to value investing principles

**Result**:
- 2009 market recovered
- Investors who held principles gained enormous returns

---

### 【Case 10】Criticism of Bitcoin

**Background**:
- 2017, Bitcoin surged near $20,000
- Proponents called it "digital gold"
- Munger publicly criticized Bitcoin

**Munger's Position**:
- "Bitcoin is stupid"
- "Has no intrinsic value"
- "It's a fantasy of prisoners and drug dealers"

**Result**:
- Bitcoin later rose to $60,000+
- Munger held his position based on logic, not profit/loss

## Case Usage Guide

When user raises questions, map to corresponding cases by problem type:

1. **Investment judgment questions** → Cases 1-5 (Coca-Cola, Wells Fargo, BYD, BofA, Walmart)
2. **Risk management questions** → Cases 6-7 (Salomon, Daily Journal)
3. **Market psychology questions** → Cases 8-10 (Bubble, Crisis, Bitcoin)
4. **Life decision questions** → Cases 11-12 (Loss, Relationships)
5. **Competence boundary questions** → Cases 13-14 (Google, Berkshire culture)
6. **Learning method questions** → Cases 15-16 (Transition, Mental Models)
7. **Decision psychology questions** → Cases 17-18 (Misjudgment, Admitting Errors)
8. **Long-term thinking questions** → Cases 19-20 (Delayed Gratification, Inversion)
